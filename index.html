About Me

I’m passionate about the intersection of language models and society, exploring both their practical applications and broader implications. My latest work focuses on LLMs in digital humanities, with a particular interest in efficient fine-tuning and domain-specific adaptations.

Beyond that, I'm interested in computational musicology, combining my background in musicology with data-driven approaches to analyze and interpret music. I enjoy working on projects that bridge the gap between machine learning, text analysis, and cultural heritage.

Writing Scholarly Footnotes with LLMs

The Heinrich Bullinger Correspondence is an Edition project that has been going on since the 1970s. To this day 3'000 out of a total of 12'000 letters have been edited. Considering the substantial time and effort the edition project takes, maybe we can enhance this project with the help of LLMs? This was my Masters's Thesis. Spoiler alert, no LLM can write detailed historical commentary as well as humans (yet). But my models are better than the generic Chat-GPT, despite being considerably smaller. Your can find them Here.

50 years of editorial Practice

Part of my Masters's Thesis was analyzing the editorial approaches. With the Heinrich Bullinger Correspondence. Over the 50 years of the editorial process, scholarly commentary might be less consistent in content as one might assume. We published this work at the DHd 2025 in Bielefeld, you can find a version of it here.
